Artifact 1: Design Thinking Lesson Plan for Creative Problem-Solving

Rationale

This artifact is a comprehensive lesson plan developed during the EDIN program that applies the Stanford d.school's Design Thinking framework to a K-12 classroom setting. The lesson was designed to guide students through the five stages of design thinking—Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test—as they addressed a real community challenge identified through stakeholder interviews. This artifact was selected because it exemplifies PLO 1 by demonstrating how structured creative processes can be embedded into instructional practice to foster innovation, empathy, and collaborative problem-solving among learners.

The lesson plan integrates multiple pedagogical strategies, including collaborative brainstorming, rapid prototyping with low-cost materials, and peer feedback cycles. It also incorporates technology tools such as digital mind-mapping software and multimedia presentation platforms, enabling students to express their creative ideas through diverse modalities. The design reflects a deliberate effort to move beyond traditional instructional approaches and create a learning experience that cultivates both divergent and convergent thinking skills.

EDIN Program Coursework

Design Thinking Lesson Plan for Creative Problem-Solving

Sitorabonu Yuldosheva | MA EDIN | Instructional Design Course

Subject Area Interdisciplinary (Science, Social Studies, ELA)
Grade Level Grades 6–8 (Adaptable K–12)
Duration 5 sessions (45–60 minutes each)
Framework Stanford d.school Design Thinking (5 Stages)
Essential Question How can we use creative problem-solving to address a real challenge in our school community?
Learning Objectives
  • Students will practice empathic inquiry through structured stakeholder interviews
  • Students will define a problem statement using the "How Might We..." framework
  • Students will generate multiple creative solutions through divergent thinking activities
  • Students will build and test low-fidelity prototypes with iterative feedback cycles
  • Students will present innovative solutions using multimedia formats

Technology Integration
  • Miro/Padlet: Collaborative digital mind-mapping and idea organization
  • Canva/Google Slides: Multimedia presentation creation
  • Flipgrid: Video reflection and peer feedback
  • Google Forms: Stakeholder interview data collection
Assessment Strategy

Formative: Observation checklists, exit tickets, peer feedback rubrics
Summative: Design Thinking Portfolio + Solution Presentation Rubric (creativity, feasibility, empathy, collaboration)

Differentiation: Multiple entry points (visual, kinesthetic, verbal); flexible grouping; choice in prototype medium; scaffolded "How Might We" templates for emerging learners; extension challenges for advanced learners.
