Section 3: Team Leaders and Learning Facilitators

Developing competencies as design team leaders and learning facilitators

Program Learning Outcome 3

PLO 3: Develop competencies as design team leaders and learning facilitators.

Professional Introduction

Section 3 of this portfolio demonstrates my growth as a collaborative leader and learning facilitator through projects that required me to coordinate team efforts, design inquiry-based learning experiences, and leverage technology to engage diverse learners. The artifacts presented here—the Smart Learners WebQuest and the accompanying WebQuest Presentation—represent a sustained effort to apply design thinking principles in a collaborative context, moving from conceptualization to implementation while managing the complexities of team-based instructional design.

These projects challenged me to step into leadership roles that demanded both creative vision and practical coordination. I learned that effective learning facilitation is not about having all the answers but about creating structures, asking the right questions, and empowering team members to contribute their unique strengths to a shared goal. The experience reinforced my understanding that PLO 3—developing competencies as design team leaders and learning facilitators—is realized through authentic, collaborative practice rather than theoretical study alone.

Artifact 1: Smart Learners WebQuest — Summary & Rationale

Professional Summary

The Smart Learners WebQuest is an interactive, web-based inquiry activity that I designed and developed as part of the MA EDIN program. Built using modern web technologies and deployed at webquest-smart-learners.vercel.app, this project translates the WebQuest model originally described by Dodge (1995) into a contemporary, technology-rich format that engages learners in structured online investigation. The WebQuest guides students through a series of tasks that require them to locate, evaluate, and synthesize information from curated web resources, culminating in a collaborative product that demonstrates their understanding.

This artifact is particularly significant for Section 3 because the design process required me to exercise both creative and organizational leadership. I coordinated the alignment of learning objectives with task design, curated appropriate web resources, and structured the inquiry process to scaffold student thinking from lower-order recall to higher-order analysis and synthesis. The project demonstrates competency in learning facilitation because it positions the educator not as the primary knowledge source but as the architect of an inquiry experience that empowers learners to construct understanding through guided exploration.

Graduate-Level Rationale

The Smart Learners WebQuest demonstrates design thinking in action: I empathized with learner needs by considering the diverse digital literacy levels and interests of the target audience, defined the instructional problem by identifying gaps in student engagement with traditional research assignments, ideated multiple approaches to scaffolded inquiry, prototyped the WebQuest using web development tools, and tested the design through iterative review and feedback. This process aligns directly with the design thinking framework articulated by Brown (2009) and reflects the kind of human-centered instructional design that PLO 3 envisions.

The WebQuest also demonstrates my competency in technology integration at the Redefinition level of the SAMR framework (Puentedura, 2013). Rather than simply digitizing a traditional research assignment, the WebQuest creates a learning experience that would be inconceivable without technology—students navigate curated digital resources in real time, collaborate through embedded interactive elements, and produce multimedia artifacts that demonstrate their understanding in ways that a paper-based assignment could not support. This level of technology integration reflects the ISTE Educator Standard 5 (Designer), which calls on educators to "design authentic, learner-driven activities and environments that recognize and accommodate learner variability" (ISTE, 2017).

Artifact 2: WebQuest Presentation — Summary & Rationale

Professional Summary

The WebQuest Presentation is a comprehensive overview of the pedagogical design, theoretical foundations, and implementation strategy for the Smart Learners WebQuest. This presentation was developed to communicate the project's rationale to academic and professional audiences, demonstrating my ability to articulate instructional design decisions using scholarly language and evidence-based reasoning. The presentation covers the learning objectives, the inquiry-based pedagogical approach, the role of technology in scaffolding student investigation, and the assessment strategies used to evaluate student learning.

Graduate-Level Rationale

Creating the WebQuest Presentation required me to exercise a different dimension of leadership: the ability to communicate complex instructional design decisions clearly and persuasively to colleagues and stakeholders. Effective learning facilitators must not only design quality learning experiences but also advocate for their pedagogical choices by grounding them in research and professional standards (Darling-Hammond et al., 2020). The presentation demonstrates my ability to connect the WebQuest design to established frameworks including Dodge's (1995) WebQuest model, the SAMR framework, and the ISTE Educator Standards, making visible the theoretical reasoning that informed each design decision.

This artifact also reflects collaborative leadership because the presentation was developed to facilitate professional dialogue about inquiry-based learning and technology integration. By sharing the design rationale and inviting feedback from peers, I modeled the kind of transparent, evidence-based professional communication that characterizes effective design team leadership. The OECD Learning Compass 2030 (OECD, 2019) emphasizes the importance of collaborative agency—the ability to work with others toward shared goals—and this presentation artifact represents my contribution to building that collaborative culture within the MA EDIN learning community.

Reflection: Team Leaders and Learning Facilitators

PLO 3: Develop competencies as design team leaders and learning facilitators.

WHAT

For Section 3 of my Capstone E-Portfolio, I developed two interconnected artifacts that demonstrate my competency as a design team leader and learning facilitator: the Smart Learners WebQuest and the accompanying WebQuest Presentation. The Smart Learners WebQuest is an interactive, web-based inquiry activity that I designed, built, and deployed using modern web technologies. It guides learners through a structured investigation process, requiring them to locate, evaluate, and synthesize information from curated online resources. The WebQuest was developed following Dodge's (1995) original model but adapted for contemporary digital environments, incorporating responsive design, multimedia elements, and scaffolded tasks that progress from guided exploration to independent analysis.

The WebQuest Presentation serves as the scholarly companion to the live WebQuest, articulating the pedagogical rationale, theoretical foundations, learning objectives, and assessment strategies that informed the project. Together, these artifacts represent a complete design cycle—from identifying a learning need, through research-informed design, to implementation and professional communication.

The design process was collaborative in nature. I led the conceptualization and development phases, making decisions about task structure, resource curation, and technology selection. I sought feedback from peers throughout the process, incorporating their insights into iterative revisions of both the WebQuest content and the visual presentation. This collaborative approach was not incidental but intentional—I recognized that effective instructional design benefits from diverse perspectives and that the process of soliciting and integrating feedback is itself an act of learning facilitation.

SO WHAT

The significance of these artifacts extends well beyond the technical skills involved in building a website or creating a presentation. At their core, these projects represent my development as a leader who can orchestrate complex instructional design processes while maintaining focus on learner needs. Brown (2009) described design thinking as a fundamentally human-centered approach to innovation—one that begins with empathy, moves through disciplined creativity, and culminates in solutions that are both functional and meaningful. The Smart Learners WebQuest embodies this approach: every design decision, from the task sequence to the resource selection to the user interface, was informed by a deep consideration of who the learners are, what they need, and how technology can best support their inquiry.

This experience also deepened my understanding of what it means to facilitate learning rather than direct it. Kolb (2015) argued that learning is most powerful when it involves a cycle of concrete experience, reflective observation, abstract conceptualization, and active experimentation. The WebQuest is designed to support precisely this cycle—students engage directly with web-based resources (concrete experience), reflect on what they have found (reflective observation), synthesize their findings into a coherent framework (abstract conceptualization), and apply their understanding to create an original product (active experimentation). By designing this learning cycle into the WebQuest structure, I was facilitating learning without controlling it—creating the conditions for student-driven inquiry while providing sufficient scaffolding to ensure productive engagement.

My leadership development through this project was significant. I learned that collaborative leadership in instructional design requires a willingness to be both visionary and flexible—to hold a clear sense of the learning goals while remaining open to unexpected ideas and alternative approaches. The ISTE Educator Standard 4 (Collaborator) calls on educators to "dedicate time to collaborate with both colleagues and students to improve practice, discover and share resources and ideas, and solve problems" (ISTE, 2017). The WebQuest project provided me with an authentic context for developing this collaborative disposition, and the presentation artifact demonstrates my ability to share instructional design work with the broader professional community.

Furthermore, the creative dimensions of this project were considerable. Designing an interactive WebQuest required me to think beyond traditional assignment formats and envision a learning experience that was visually engaging, cognitively demanding, and technologically sophisticated. Henriksen et al. (2016) argued that creativity and technology must be integrated systemically in education, not treated as separate domains. The Smart Learners WebQuest represents this integration—creativity informed every aspect of the design, from the inquiry tasks to the visual layout, and technology served as the medium through which that creativity was expressed and made accessible to learners.

NOW WHAT

Moving forward, the competencies I developed through the Smart Learners WebQuest and Presentation will shape my professional practice in several important ways. First, I plan to continue designing technology-enhanced, inquiry-based learning experiences that position students as active investigators rather than passive consumers of information. The WebQuest model, when adapted for contemporary digital environments, offers a powerful framework for structured inquiry that is rigorous, engaging, and accessible to diverse learners (Dodge, 1995). I intend to develop a series of WebQuests across different content areas, building a library of inquiry-based resources that other educators can adapt and use in their own classrooms.

Second, I will carry forward the collaborative leadership practices that this project helped me develop. I learned that the best instructional design emerges from collective thinking—from the willingness to share ideas early, invite honest feedback, and revise iteratively. Schön (1983) described this kind of professional practice as "reflection-in-action"—the ability to think critically about what one is doing while doing it, adjusting course in response to emerging insights. I will seek out and create professional learning communities where this kind of collaborative, reflective design work is the norm rather than the exception.

Third, this experience has strengthened my commitment to technology integration that genuinely transforms learning rather than merely replicating traditional practices in digital form. The OECD Learning Compass 2030 (OECD, 2019) envisions educators who help learners develop the agency, competencies, and values needed to shape a better future. I see technology-enhanced inquiry as one pathway toward this vision—when students learn to navigate information landscapes critically, collaborate with diverse peers, and create original knowledge products, they are developing the very competencies that the OECD framework describes.

Finally, I recognize that leadership in education is an ongoing process of growth, not a destination. The Smart Learners WebQuest was a significant milestone in my development, but it also revealed areas where I can continue to improve—particularly in designing more differentiated scaffolding for learners at different levels and in developing more robust assessment strategies for inquiry-based work. I am committed to pursuing these areas of growth with the same spirit of curiosity, collaboration, and evidence-based practice that the MA EDIN program has cultivated in me.
