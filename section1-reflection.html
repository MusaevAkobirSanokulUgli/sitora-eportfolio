Reflection: Creativity, Innovation & Instructional Problem-Solving

PLO 1: Promote, inspire, and cultivate creativity and innovative thinking for learning, service, and leadership.

WHAT

The two artifacts presented in Section 1 represent my engagement with creativity and innovation as foundational elements of effective educational practice. The first artifact, a Design Thinking Lesson Plan, demonstrates my ability to translate the Stanford d.school's five-stage design thinking process—Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test—into a structured, classroom-ready instructional experience (Brown, 2009). The lesson was designed to engage learners in addressing a real community problem through collaborative, human-centered inquiry, positioning creativity not as an innate talent but as a teachable, iterative process (IDEO, 2012). The second artifact, a Concept Map of Creativity Frameworks, represents a scholarly synthesis of major creativity theories, including Csikszentmihalyi's (2013) Systems Model, Sternberg's (2006) Investment Theory, Amabile's (1996) Componential Model, and Torrance's (1972) creative thinking processes. This concept map connects theoretical constructs to practical classroom applications, serving as both a personal learning tool and a professional resource for educators seeking to ground their creative pedagogy in research.

Together, these artifacts demonstrate that cultivating creativity in education requires both theoretical depth and practical application. The lesson plan shows how to structure creative experiences for learners, while the concept map reveals the scholarly foundation upon which such instruction is built. Both artifacts were developed during the MA EDIN program and reflect the program's emphasis on integrating theory with practice in the pursuit of innovative educational solutions.

SO WHAT

These artifacts matter because they demonstrate mastery of PLO 1—the ability to promote, inspire, and cultivate creativity and innovative thinking for learning, service, and leadership. In an era defined by rapid technological change and complex global challenges, educators must prepare learners to think creatively and adaptively (Robinson, 2011; Pink, 2006). The traditional model of education, which prioritizes convergent thinking and standardized outcomes, is insufficient for developing the creative capacities that learners need to thrive in the 21st century (Sawyer, 2012). The design thinking lesson plan directly addresses this challenge by providing a structured framework through which students can practice divergent thinking, empathic inquiry, and iterative problem-solving.

The significance of these artifacts extends beyond individual classroom practice. Research consistently demonstrates that creativity is not a fixed trait but a set of skills and dispositions that can be developed through intentional instructional design (Beghetto & Kaufman, 2014). As Henriksen et al. (2016) argue, integrating creativity and technology in education requires educators who can model innovative thinking and create environments where creative risk-taking is valued. The concept map artifact reflects this principle by demonstrating my own engagement in creative scholarly synthesis—the process of identifying connections across diverse theoretical frameworks and representing them in a novel visual format. This is precisely the kind of interdisciplinary, innovative thinking that the ISTE Educator Standards (Standard 5: Designer; Standard 1: Learner) call for in contemporary educators (International Society for Technology in Education [ISTE], 2017).

Furthermore, the UNESCO Teacher Competency Standards emphasize that effective educators must possess not only pedagogical skills but also a deep understanding of the theoretical foundations that inform their practice (UNESCO, 2019). The concept map demonstrates this competency by showing how multiple creativity theories converge to inform instructional decision-making. As Amabile (1996) noted, creativity emerges at the intersection of domain-relevant skills, creativity-relevant processes, and intrinsic task motivation—a framework that has direct implications for how educators design learning experiences. The design thinking lesson plan operationalizes this insight by structuring activities that build domain knowledge (empathize and define stages), engage creativity-relevant processes (ideate and prototype stages), and sustain intrinsic motivation through authentic, community-connected challenges.

NOW WHAT

Moving forward, the insights gained from developing these artifacts will fundamentally shape my professional practice. First, I will continue to integrate design thinking methodologies into my instructional repertoire, not as a one-time activity but as a recurring framework for structuring creative learning experiences across content areas (Brown, 2009; IDEO, 2012). I plan to develop a series of design thinking units that progress in complexity, allowing learners to build their creative capacities over time. This approach aligns with Torrance's (1972) finding that creative thinking skills improve with sustained practice and deliberate instruction.

Second, I will use the concept map as a living document, continuously updating it as new research on creativity in education emerges. This practice reflects the ISTE Educator Standard 1 (Learner), which calls on educators to "set professional learning goals to explore and apply pedagogical approaches made possible by technology" (ISTE, 2017, p. 1). By maintaining a current understanding of creativity research, I can ensure that my instructional design decisions are evidence-based and responsive to the evolving needs of my learners.

Third, I intend to share these resources with colleagues through professional learning communities, contributing to a culture of innovation within my educational institution. Csikszentmihalyi (2013) emphasized that creativity does not occur in isolation but within systems that include individuals, domains of knowledge, and fields of practice. By sharing the design thinking lesson plan and creativity frameworks concept map, I can contribute to building a professional field that values and supports creative instruction. This commitment to collaborative knowledge-sharing is central to PLO 1's emphasis on promoting creativity for "learning, service, and leadership."

Finally, these artifacts have deepened my awareness of the need to create classroom environments where creative risk-taking is psychologically safe. Beghetto and Kaufman (2014) stress that creativity flourishes in contexts where learners feel supported in taking intellectual risks without fear of judgment. As I move forward in my career, I will prioritize building trust and establishing norms that encourage learners to explore unconventional ideas, make mistakes, and learn from iterative feedback. This commitment reflects not only PLO 1 but also the broader mission of the MA EDIN program: to develop educators who lead with creativity, empathy, and innovation.
